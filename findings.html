Featured Artifact: HOLC Grade Boundaries and Present-Day Health Outcomes

What This Visualization Shows

The interactive map accompanying Helen C.S. Meier's essay "Redlining and Health" illustrates the stark gap in health outcomes between census tracts that contained A-graded and D-graded HOLC areas. Meier reports that a census tract containing an A-graded area today shows a projected life expectancy of 86 years, an asthma prevalence of 8%, a diabetes prevalence of 8%, and a mental health problems prevalence of 8%. By contrast, a census tract containing a D-graded ("hazardous") area shows a projected life expectancy of just 68 years, an asthma prevalence of 14%, a diabetes prevalence of 21%, and a mental health problems prevalence of 22%. As Meier summarizes: "Evidence suggests that HOLC redlined areas are associated with many present-day health outcomes, including mortality, pre-term birth, stage of cancer diagnosis, COVID-19 infection burden, infant mortality, poor mental health, poor physical health, self-rated health, cardiovascular disease, hypertension and diabetes prevalence."

What This Visualization Cannot Show

Meier is careful to note that redlining "certainly was not the only determinant of inequality" and that the mechanisms by which historic redlining influences health "remain largely unstudied." The visualization does not capture other historic policies — urban renewal, restrictive covenants, steering practices — that also shaped neighborhood disinvestment over time. Meier also cautions that "each city has its own story": the racist logic encoded in HOLC area descriptions differed between cities (explicitly anti-Black in Richmond, Virginia; predominantly anti-immigrant and anti-Semitic in Milwaukee, Wisconsin), and local context is essential to interpreting any single map.

About This Artifact

This page reproduces a screenshot of the Leaflet map embedded in Meier's essay on the Mapping Inequality platform maintained by the University of Richmond Digital Scholarship Lab. The underlying data, visualization, and interpretive framework are the work of Meier and the Mapping Inequality team. The screenshot was captured for use as a static reference in this course project; the original interactive version, including hover data for individual census tracts, is available at the URL above.
