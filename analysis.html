What This Visualization Shows

This visualization features key moments of Irish–Indigenous connection, beginning with the 1847 Choctaw Nation donation during the Great Famine and extending to modern memorials, cultural exchanges, and acts of reciprocal support, including ongoing commemorative projects and educational initiatives. Visitors should notice that these interactions are not isolated events, but part of a continuing relationship of remembrance and mutual recognition that evolves over time through monuments, storytelling, and cultural exchange.

However, this visualization does not fully capture the emotional depth, oral histories, and lived experiences behind these events, which require additional context beyond what a digital map or timeline can display.

17th–18th Centuries

Early Irish Settlement on Indigenous Lands

Irish settlers were among those who arrived in North America through regions that were historically Indigenous lands.

Jan. 1, 1801

Acts of Union

The Acts of Union merged Great Britain and Ireland, dissolving the Irish Parliament and placing Ireland under direct rule from Westminster.

Sept. 7, 1830

Treaty of Dancing Rabbit Creek

The Choctaw Nation ceded lands in Mississippi and were relocated to Indian Territory. Irish immigrant Daniel McCurtain, married into the Choctaw Nation, was present. His descendants later became tribal leaders.

1830–1850

Trail of Tears

The forced removal of approximately 60,000–100,000 Native Americans displaced the Cherokee, Muscogee, Seminole, Chickasaw, and Choctaw nations. Thousands died from disease, exposure, and starvation during the journey.

1845–1852

Great Irish Famine

Crop failures caused by potato blight devastated Ireland. Over one million died and two million emigrated. Colonial policies and economic systems worsened the crisis.

March 23, 1847

Choctaw Donation

The Choctaw Nation raised $170 (over $6,000 today) to aid Irish famine victims in Midleton, County Cork which serves an enduring symbol of transatlantic solidarity.

1850s

Irish Missionary Activity

Irish missionaries traveled to Indigenous communities, attempting to convert them to Catholicism.

1861–1865

American Civil War

Around 200,000 Irish-born soldiers fought in the Civil War, mostly for the Union. Native nations were divided in allegiance, leading to further displacement and loss of sovereignty.

1860s–1890s

Cultural Exchange & Advocacy

Irish immigrants and Indigenous leaders shared aid strategies, stories, songs, and crafts, fostering hybrid cultural expressions.

1861–1921

James Mooney’s Ethnographic Work

Irish-American ethnologist James Mooney documented Indigenous oral histories and traditions, especially among the Cherokee. He treated Native cultures as legitimate and preserved sacred knowledge often inaccessible to outsiders.

Oct. 18, 1919

Éamon de Valera & Ojibwe Nation

Éamon de Valera was named an honorary Ojibwe chieftain, symbolizing solidarity between Irish and Native American struggles.

May 1990

First Famine Walk

Choctaw leaders participated in Ireland’s Famine Walk, commemorating Irish suffering and shared history.

May 1992

Commemoration of Trail of Tears

Irish participants traveled to the United States to retrace the Trail of Tears alongside the Choctaw Nation.

May 29, 1992

Dublin Plaque Unveiled

A plaque honoring the Choctaw donation was installed at Mansion House in Dublin.

June 1995

Mary Robinson Visit

Irish President Mary Robinson visited the Choctaw Nation and was named an honorary chief.

June 2017

Kindred Spirits Sculpture

The Kindred Spirits sculpture in Midleton commemorates the Choctaw donation and enduring friendship.

March 2018

Scholarship Program Announced

Taoiseach Leo Varadkar announced scholarships for Choctaw students to study in Ireland.

2020–2021

COVID-19 Solidarity

Irish donors raised over $3 million for Navajo and Hopi communities, inspired by the Choctaw’s 1847 donation.

Aug. 30, 2024

Eternal Heart Monument

The Choctaw Nation unveiled a companion monument in Oklahoma celebrating Irish–Choctaw friendship.

July 17, 2025

Gift Exchange Continues

Navajo and Hopi representatives gifted a hand-woven rug to Ireland, symbolizing ongoing gratitude and connection.

More On the Map

This map invites viewers to trace moments of Irish–Indigenous connection across time and space, beginning with the 1847 aid sent by the Choctaw Nation during the Great Famine and extending into later memorials, commemorations, and cultural exchanges. Users should click on map markers to explore each location and notice how every point represents a moment of solidarity, remembrance, or shared history rather than an isolated event.

Within the broader project, this map acts as a bridge between history and memory. It shows how relationships formed in moments of crisis continue to shape cultural exchange and public commemoration today, reinforcing the project’s central argument that Irish and Indigenous histories are interconnected through ongoing acts of recognition.
