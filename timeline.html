Aims of Timeline

Each traces key moments of Irish–Indigenous connection, beginning with the 1847 Choctaw Nation donation during the Great Famine and extending into modern memorials, cultural exchanges, and acts of reciprocal support. These events demonstrate that solidarity is not isolated but ongoing, shaped through memory, commemoration, and cultural exchange over time.

However, they cannot fully represent the emotional depth, oral histories, and lived experiences behind these events. It should be understood as a starting point for interpretation rather than a complete historical record.

The timeline is planned to include images and be linked to sources used for this project. This have not been included yet.

17th–18th Centuries

Early Irish Settlement on Indigenous Lands

Irish settlers arrived in North America on lands historically inhabited by Indigenous nations.

Jan. 1, 1801

Acts of Union

The Acts of Union dissolved the Irish Parliament and placed Ireland under British rule.

Sept. 7, 1830

Treaty of Dancing Rabbit Creek

The Choctaw Nation ceded lands in Mississippi and were forced into relocation.

1845–1852

Great Irish Famine

Over one million died and millions emigrated due to famine and colonial policy failures.

March 23, 1847

Choctaw Donation

The Choctaw Nation raised funds to aid Irish famine victims, becoming a lasting symbol of solidarity.

June 2017

Kindred Spirits Sculpture

Midleton, Ireland monument honoring Choctaw generosity and transatlantic friendship.

2020–2021

COVID-19 Solidarity

Irish donors raised funds for Navajo and Hopi communities during the pandemic.
