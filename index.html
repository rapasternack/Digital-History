Irish–Indigenous Interactions

A digital history of solidarity, colonialism, and connection across continents

Introduction

In 1847, during the Great Irish Famine, the Choctaw Nation—still recovering from forced removal along the Trail of Tears—sent aid to Ireland. Despite their own hardship, they raised $170 to support famine relief across the Atlantic.

This project explores Irish–Indigenous interactions beyond this moment, examining shared histories of colonialism, migration, memory, and solidarity.

Key Themes

Solidarity & Survival

The Choctaw donation represents transnational empathy in the 19th century.

Colonialism & Displacement

Both Irish and Indigenous peoples experienced imperial control and land loss.

Migration & Identity

Irish immigrants became settlers, complicating narratives of oppression.

Memory & Commemoration

These histories continue through memorials and global connections today.

Why This History Matters

The Choctaw–Irish relationship continues through memorials, cultural exchange, and acts of mutual aid. These histories challenge national narratives and reveal how empathy can transcend borders.
