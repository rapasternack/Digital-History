Irish-Indigenous Interactions

A digital exploration of historical connections between Ireland and Indigenous nations.

Introduction

In 1847, during the height of the Great Famine in Ireland, the Choctaw Nation, located in what is now Oklahoma, sent a remarkable donation to aid those suffering thousands of miles away. This gesture of solidarity, along with other moments of exchange, activism, and cultural connection between Irish and Indigenous peoples, reveals a shared history of resilience, empathy, and transatlantic relationships.

This digital project explores these interactions through maps, timelines, and stories, highlighting the people, events, and themes that shaped this chapter of history.

Historical Context

The mid-19th century was a period of profound hardship and transformation on both sides of the Atlantic. In Ireland, the Great Famine (1845–1852) caused mass starvation, displacement, and social upheaval. Meanwhile, Indigenous nations in the United States were facing their own crises, including forced relocations and cultural disruption following policies like the Indian Removal Act.

Amid these challenges, a story of transatlantic solidarity emerges. In 1847, the Choctaw Nation, having recently endured the Trail of Tears, raised funds to support Irish famine relief. This act of generosity, along with other Irish-Indigenous interactions, reflects a broader history of shared resilience, empathy, and mutual recognition across continents.

Beyond aid, these interactions challenge common narratives of isolation between Indigenous communities and European populations, uncovering an interconnected history.
