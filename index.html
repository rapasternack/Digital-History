Eamon De Valera (president of Ireland) shakes hands with members of Ojibwe tribe in Wisconsin during a 1919 tour of the U.S. Source: Getty Images / Hulton Archive

Introduction

In 1847, during the Great Irish Famine, the Choctaw Nation, who were still recovering from forced removal along the Trail of Tears, sent aid to Ireland. Despite their own hardship, they raised $170 to support famine relief across the Atlantic.

This project explores Irish–Indigenous interactions beyond this moment, examining shared histories of colonialism, migration, memory, and solidarity.

Key Themes

Solidarity & Survival

The Choctaw donation represents transnational empathy in the 19th century.

Colonialism & Displacement

Both Irish and Indigenous peoples experienced imperial control and land loss.

Memory & Commemoration

These histories continue through memorials and global connections today.

Why This History Matters

The Irish-Indigenous relationships continues through memorials, cultural exchange, and acts of mutual aid today. These histories challenge national narratives and reveal how solidarity can transcend borders.
