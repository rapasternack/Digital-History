Project Overview

This project asks how the Home Owners' Loan Corporation's 1930s neighborhood grading system shaped the long-term spatial distribution of wealth and public services in one mid-sized American city. The HOLC maps classified neighborhoods as A through D — "best" to "hazardous" — using criteria that explicitly encoded race and immigration into the geography of creditworthiness.

The project uses a small dataset derived from digitized HOLC boundary data and census records to examine the relationship between 1930s grades and later outcomes in public school funding, green space, and median household income. One cleaned dataset and one map produced earlier in the semester form the core of the public presentation here.

Research Question

To what extent do HOLC grade boundaries from the 1930s predict present-day disparities in public investment at the neighborhood level — and what does that persistence suggest about how historical records encode inequality?

Why It Matters

Redlining has been extensively documented at the national level. This project works at a smaller scale to ask whether the same pattern holds locally, and whether digital history methods can make that argument legible to a non-specialist reader. The goal is not to produce a definitive study but to demonstrate that the historical record — even a partial one — can be used to make a visible spatial argument.
