Interface Sketches

Before building any pages, I drew schematic sketches of each page on paper. The goal was to decide, for each page, two things: what the page should reveal to a visitor, and what it should guide them to do. Using low-fidelity sketches allowed me to focus on information hierarchy and user flow without being distracted by CSS styling or typography early on.

Design Decisions: The "Reveal vs. Guide" Framework

Intended visitor: A general public reader with no specialist background in urban history or redlining — curious, literate, unfamiliar with HOLC grading as a concept. Not a researcher; not a student in this course. Someone who might follow a link from a local history blog or a civic data project.

1. Home Page: Context Before Question

Reveals: The historical context of HOLC grading and the specific geographic scope of this study.

Guides: The visitor is guided to understand the why before the what. By placing the formal research question after the overview, the layout ensures that the visitor has the necessary historical vocabulary to understand the question's significance. This order is calibrated for the intended visitor — a general reader who needs the context before the technical question makes sense.

2. Findings Page: Primary Source Before Argument

Reveals: The central spatial argument — the visual correlation between 1930s grades and 2020 income levels.

Guides: The design guides the visitor to perform their own visual analysis of the map before encountering the written argument. For a general reader unfamiliar with redlining, seeing the map first creates an open question; the interpretive text then answers it. This sequence would be less important for a specialist who already knows the argument.

3. Sustainability Page: Documentation as Design

Reveals: The technical structure of the site, including file dependencies and data sources.

Guides: Unlike the other pages, this does not guide the intended visitor — it guides a different user entirely: a future researcher, archivist, or maintainer. The shift to a simple, unadorned list format signals that the site has changed audience and purpose.
